Designing a Mixed Signal Printed Circuit Board - Part 1 (Introduction)

January 8, 2024

Introduction

Throughout my electrical engineering degree, I've come to realize the indispensable value of hands-on experience. Theory and lectures provide the foundation, but it's the application that solidifies understanding and uncovers the depth of the subject matter. This belief prompted me to enroll in Phil's Lab mixed-signal PCB course, a decision that marked the beginning of a journey beyond the classroom. In this series of blog posts, I'll be documenting this journey, detailing the learnings and challenges encountered while developing a "USB-powered, single-channel, low frequency signal analyzer and signal generator."

Project Goals and Specifications

The objective was clear: to create a device that's both powered by and communicates through a USB 2.0 interface, targeting the audio band frequency range of 20 Hz to 20 kHz. This bandwidth choice influenced our data rate requirements, leading us to settle on USB Full Speed (FS) mode for its adequacy in handling our projected data throughput without the need for higher transfer rates. By aiming for a sampling rate of 40 kHz—double our highest frequency of interest to adhere to the Nyquist theorem—and opting for an ADC with a 12-bit resolution, we calculated a modest data transfer need of approximately 0.5 Mbits/s.

Selecting the Right Components

For the brain of our project, we chose the STM32F103C8T6 microcontroller (MCU) for its simplicity and adequacy in processing power. Its built-in SPI communication capability perfectly matches our needs for interfacing with the ADC and DAC, eliminating the necessity for more complex processing power. Power management required careful consideration; most components on our board operate at +3.3V, necessitating a switch from the USB's +5V supply. This was achieved using a buck converter (TLV62569DBV) for digital circuitry and an LDO regulator (HT7533-1) for analog circuitry, ensuring stable and efficient power distribution across the board.

Core Components:

  • MCU: STM32F103C8T6, chosen for its simplicity and SPI capabilities.
  • LDO Regulator: HT7533-1, for stable +3.3V power to analog components.
  • Switching Regulator: TLV62569DBV, stepping down +5V to +3.3V for digital components.
  • DAC: DAC7563SDGSR, a 12-bit single-ended digital-to-analog converter.
  • ADC: ADC141S626CIMM, a 14-bit differential analog-to-digital converter.
